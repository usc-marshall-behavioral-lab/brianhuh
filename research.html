Aviation Human Factors

Where Behavioral Science Meets the Technology That Runs Operations

Human factors is the study of how people, organizations, and technology interact — particularly in high-stakes environments where decisions under pressure determine outcomes. In aviation, it asks: why do accidents happen even when the aircraft is working and the crew is trained? The answer is almost always behavioral and organizational.

I study aviation accidents and incidents as a behavioral scientist, not as a technical safety auditor. The questions I bring are behavioral: what organizational dynamics, communication patterns, and decision biases contribute to what goes wrong — and what does that mean for how safety systems should be designed?

Increasingly, those systems involve AI and leading operational technologies — sensors, radar, lidar, and the AI platforms that synthesize their data into investigative and situational awareness tools. How safety professionals develop trust in these technologies, how they calibrate reliance under uncertainty, and what behavioral conditions make adoption succeed or fail are central questions in this research program.

The objective is applied: derive principles from consumer and organizational behavior that translate into better-designed technologies, better-trained professionals, and measurable improvements in operational efficiency and safety outcomes.

A structural equation modeling study of 269 aviation safety professionals examined what predicts trust in AI tools within investigative practice. A single factor accounted for nearly half the variance in trust scores. The dominant predictor was not demographic — not age, seniority, or organizational affiliation — but the degree to which investigators maintained active professional engagement with their field.

Core Implication for ISASI Guidance

Organizational factors are non-significant. Trust calibration programs cannot rely on top-down institutional interventions alone — professional engagement is the lever.

Professional engagement interventions work broadly across all subgroups. Management/Safety roles show the strongest effect and represent a priority cohort for early adoption programs.

Composite Trust Model · ISASI 2025 Denver

Presented at ISASI Annual Seminar 2025: AI trust among aviation professionals is not a single variable. It is a dynamic composite of five distinct psychological mechanisms — each requiring a different intervention strategy.

Click any component to expand

Curiosity +
The intrinsic motivation to explore AI tools out of genuine interest, independent of organizational pressure. Investigators high in curiosity seek out AI applications on their own initiative — predicting higher baseline trust and adoption readiness.
Learned Risk +
Calibrated wariness derived from direct experience with past technology failures or misapplication. Distinct from simple resistance — learned risk reflects informed skepticism that can actually improve the quality of AI adoption by prompting verification behaviors rather than blind reliance.
Perceived Utility +
The investigator's confidence that a specific AI tool meaningfully improves investigative outcomes — not AI in the abstract, but this tool for this task. Perceived utility is task-specific and context-dependent, which means training programs must demonstrate concrete investigative applications rather than general capability claims.
Structural Access +
Whether the investigator is organizationally included in AI-enabled workflows — assigned to AI-assisted investigations, given access to AI platforms, included in tool evaluation. Structural access predicts trust because exposure drives familiarity; its absence explains why institutional mandates alone fail to produce adoption.
Message Framing +
How AI is presented to investigators shapes initial trust orientation. Innovation framing ("this expands what investigations can do") works better with management and senior staff. Error-prevention framing ("this catches what humans miss") works better with frontline investigators. Mismatched framing actively suppresses adoption regardless of tool quality.

Trust & Automation

Why AI Is Driving More Demand for In-Person Research — And What That Tells Us About Trust

Something counterintuitive is happening in behavioral research: as AI becomes more capable of completing online studies, researchers are increasingly turning back to in-person lab environments. The reason is straightforward but underappreciated — they need to be confident they are studying real human participants.

This shift is not primarily about methodology. It is about trust. Specifically, it is about the conditions under which a researcher can trust that the data they collect reflects genuine human cognition rather than automated response. When that trust is uncertain, the entire evidentiary foundation of behavioral science becomes shaky.

From where I sit — running a research lab that has seen in-person study hours jump 122% in a single year — this looks less like a logistical trend and more like a behavioral signal. It reveals something important about how trust in automated systems actually works: when the stakes are high enough, people revert to methods they can verify directly, even when those methods are more costly and inconvenient.

This is the core problem of automation trust. We don't need people to distrust AI — we need them to trust it appropriately. That means having clear signals for when AI output is reliable, when it isn't, and how to tell the difference. The behavioral science of trust calibration has a lot to say here. Aviation figured this out decades ago. Research methodology is learning it now.

Professional Engagement Patterns and AI Trust Among Safety Investigators

My research shows that how aviation safety professionals engage with their field — not just their technical training — predicts how they develop trust toward AI tools. What that means for AI adoption in safety-critical work.

The central finding from the ISASI survey — that professional engagement is the only significant predictor of AI trust among aviation safety investigators — raises a deeper question: what does professional engagement actually consist of, and why does it predict trust? Decision-Making

Decisions Made Without Full Information

Most consequential decisions — in aviation, in organizations, in research — happen before all the facts are in. What behavioral science reveals about how people navigate uncertainty, and how they can do it better.

Most consequential decisions — in aviation, in organizations, in research — happen before all the facts are in. Behavioral science has studied this problem extensively: how people represent uncertainty, what heuristics they rely on under time pressure, and where those heuristics systematically fail. This essay draws on research from naturalistic decision-making, signal detection theory, and organizational behavior to argue that the goal should not be to eliminate uncertainty but to improve the quality of reasoning under it.
