Marshall Behavioral Research Lab

Research support operations · Marshall Behavioral Research Lab

The Marshall Behavioral Research Lab exists to serve the full range of researchers at USC Marshall — faculty, doctoral students, and their teams — across all topics and methods. My own research focuses on aviation human factors and AI trust. The lab's mandate is institutional, not topical.

Research Lab Management

Running the Infrastructure That Makes Behavioral Research Possible

Running a research lab at scale is itself a scientific problem. The decisions that shape whether faculty can run rigorous studies — how participant pools are structured, when online experimentation serves a question and when in-person does, how archival and field data needs differ from lab studies, which technology platforms support which research designs — require deep methodological fluency, not just operational management skill.

The Marshall Behavioral Research Lab has grown from a two-department facility into USC Marshall's primary behavioral science infrastructure — supporting 60 active faculty study teams across 11 departments. I lead a team of three full-time staff and serves as the primary methodological partner for faculty and doctoral researchers who need more than a room and a scheduling system.

A significant part of that expertise is understanding the tradeoffs between online and in-person experimentation. The lab navigated a full pivot to remote methodology during COVID-19 — building out Qualtrics, Prolific, and online panel infrastructure — then deliberately shifted back toward in-person as AI-generated responses and automated participation degraded data quality in online panels. That decision required scientific judgment, not just operational adjustment.

Whether a study runs online or in person is not a logistical question — it is a scientific one. The answer shapes what can be validly concluded. Getting that right, consistently, across 60 concurrent faculty study teams, is what running a research lab at this scale actually requires.

Operational Adaptation

From Remote-First to In-Person — A Deliberate Strategic Shift

2020 – 2022 · Remote-First

During COVID-19, the lab pivoted entirely to online and remote experimentation — rebuilding participation infrastructure around Qualtrics, Prolific, and third-party online panels. This allowed research to continue uninterrupted and expanded the lab's online methodology capabilities substantially.

2023 – Present · In-Person Direction

As AI-generated responses, automated bots, and data quality degradation became endemic to online panels, the lab shifted strategic direction back toward in-person experimentation — where you can confirm who is in the room and what they are actually doing. In-person research hours increased 107.7% in FY2024 and a further 61.6% in FY2025, reaching 3,045 hours — up from 908 in FY2023. Total research output reached 11,176 hours across 163 studies in FY2024–25.

This shift is not a retreat from online methods — third-party and compensated online panels remain part of the lab's methodology portfolio. It reflects a deliberate reallocation of resources toward study designs where data quality can be controlled. The same dynamic is examined in the featured essay on AI trust and in-person research.

Technology Initiative

CORAL Platform

Coordinated Operations for Research Assistant Logistics

A custom scheduling and operations management platform developed through a formal academic partnership with the USC Viterbi School of Engineering's CSCI 401 capstone program. Designed and spec'd by the lab, built at no cost by a team of six computer science students during Fall 2025 — targeting deployment in Spring 2026.

CORAL automates scheduling and shift assignment across 30+ research assistants, enforces USC labor compliance rules (20-hour weekly caps, break requirements), coordinates simultaneous multi-room study sessions, and integrates live data from participant management platforms into a centralized dashboard. Estimated to reduce administrative workload by 5–8 hours per week.

Trust in Automation

A field session at the USC Tower open house yielded 106 complete responses in a single afternoon. The speed of engagement was notable: when presented with appropriately framed questions, professionals engage substantively with AI trust issues. The engagement pattern is consistent with findings from the ISASI survey: reluctance toward AI appears more strongly associated with uncertainty about reliability boundaries than with categorical distrust of the technology.

Field Research · Denver 2025

The ISASI Annual Seminar in Denver drew aviation safety professionals from 47 countries — one of the field's largest annual gatherings globally. What the survey data reveal about seniority and AI trust does not align with standard technology adoption models. Experience does not produce resistance — it produces calibrated skepticism. These are behaviorally distinct phenomena and require different intervention strategies.

About

I am a behavioral scientist and the manager of the Marshall Behavioral Research Lab — USC Marshall's primary research infrastructure. My day-to-day work is methodological: faculty come to me with a research question and leave with a study design, a participant sourcing strategy, and a clear answer to whether their design will produce valid evidence. I have done this across 11 departments for eight years.

The lab expertise I have developed covers the full range of behavioral research modes — in-person experimentation, online panels, field deployments, archival work, and behavioral measurement technology including eye-tracking and psychophysiology. A significant part of that expertise is knowing where each method's validity breaks down — which is why the lab's deliberate shift back toward in-person research, as AI-generated responses degraded online panel quality, was a methodological decision before it was an operational one.

I hold an M.S. in Applied Psychology from USC Dornsife, a Graduate Certificate in Business Analytics from USC Marshall, a Certificate in Performance Leadership from Cornell ILR School, and a B.A. in Psychology from Harvard. 2024 · Published
Published peer-reviewed article examining how behavioral science frameworks — organizational behavior and consumer behavior — apply to aviation human factors and safety investigations. Conducted in collaboration with the ISASI Human Factors Working Group and USC Viterbi Aviation Safety & Security Program. Study of 127 aviation safety experts worldwide. Available: lnkd.in/gT7ByF_Z
Trust-Centered Investigations: Integrating Human Factors and Behavioral Science with AI in Aviation Safety
Working paper · ISASI Human Factors Working Group · N=540 · 47 countries
2024–2025
Working paper. Multi-level study of 540 aviation safety professionals across 47 countries — 269 for individual-level SEM (EFA→CFA→SEM), 172 for organizational analysis. Primary finding: professional engagement explains 48.9% of AI trust variance (β = 0.727, p<0.001). Introduces the AI Trust Assessment Index (ATAI; α=.96, CR=.92). Key findings: HF maturity paradox (β = −0.11, informed skepticism vs. resistance), mid-career advantage (74% stronger engagement-trust relationship at 3–10 years), organizational threshold at integration level 3.5. Presented at ISASI Annual Seminar 2024 (Lisbon) and ISASI Annual Seminar 2025 (Denver).
