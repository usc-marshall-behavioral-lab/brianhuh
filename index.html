About

Brian is a behavioral scientist whose work applies organizational behavior and consumer behavior frameworks to the study of aviation accidents, incidents, and the human systems that shape operational safety. He bridges theory and practice — using behavioral science to understand why people make the decisions they do under pressure, and what that means for how organizations, technologies, and safety cultures should be designed.

For a general audience: Brian studies why people make the decisions they do in high-pressure situations — particularly in aviation, where the consequences of those decisions are consequential. He also manages the behavioral science research infrastructure at USC Marshall — the facilities, participant pool, equipment, and staff that make it possible for faculty across the school to conduct empirical research. The lab supports research across all topics and disciplines; his own research focus is aviation human factors and AI trust. He also trains the next generation of researchers, and advises on how organizations should think about AI and new technology.

He runs the Marshall Behavioral Research Lab at USC Marshall School of Business — the school's primary behavioral science research infrastructure — where he oversees 60 active faculty study teams across 11 departments, leads a team of three full-time staff, and serves as the methodological partner for faculty and doctoral researchers who need expert guidance on research design, experimentation mode, and data quality. His domain expertise spans online and in-person experimental design, archival research support, behavioral measurement technology, and participant ecosystem strategy.

He holds an M.S. in Applied Psychology from USC Dornsife, a Graduate Certificate in Business Analytics from USC Marshall, a Certificate in Performance Leadership from Cornell ILR School, and a B.A. in Psychology from Harvard. Prior to USC, he held research roles at Harvard, Columbia Business School, Chicago Booth, and Stanford.

His mentorship program has trained 139 students since 2017. Alumni have gone on to positions in tech, consulting, finance, and research — not simply because USC Marshall is on their degree, but because they worked in a real behavioral science lab, handled real data, and understand how rigorous empirical research actually works.

Human Factors in Aviation Safety: Bridging Behavioral Science and Operational Practice
ISASI Forum — Journal of the International Society of Air Safety Investigators · Vol. 57, No. 2
2024 · Published
Published peer-reviewed article examining how behavioral science frameworks — organizational behavior and consumer behavior — apply to aviation human factors and safety investigations. Conducted in collaboration with the ISASI Human Factors Working Group and USC Viterbi Aviation Safety & Security Program. Study of 127 aviation safety experts worldwide. Available: lnkd.in/gT7ByF_Z
AI Trust Among Aviation Safety Professionals — SEM Study
ISASI Human Factors Working Group needs assessment · N=269 · 49 countries
2024
Structural equation modeling study (EFA→CFA→SEM) identifying professional engagement as the dominant predictor of AI trust among aviation safety investigators (β=0.727, R²=48.9%, p<0.001). Presented at ISASI Annual Seminar 2024 (Lisbon, Portugal) and ISASI Annual Seminar 2025 (Denver, CO). Introduced composite trust model — curiosity, learned risk, perceived utility, structural access, and message framing — as distinct psychological mechanisms each requiring different intervention strategies.
