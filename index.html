Aviation Psychology & Operational Technology

Where Behavioral Science Meets the Technology That Runs Operations

Human factors is the study of how people, organizations, and technology interact — particularly in high-stakes environments where decisions under pressure determine outcomes. In aviation, it asks: why do accidents happen even when the aircraft is working and the crew is trained? The answer is almost always behavioral and organizational.

I study aviation accidents and incidents as a psychologist, not as a technical safety auditor. The questions I bring are behavioral: what organizational dynamics, communication patterns, and decision biases contribute to what goes wrong — and what does that mean for how safety systems should be designed?

Increasingly, those systems involve AI and leading operational technologies — sensors, radar, lidar, and the AI platforms that synthesize their data into investigative and situational awareness tools. How safety professionals develop trust in these technologies, how they calibrate reliance under uncertainty, and what behavioral conditions make adoption succeed or fail are central questions in this research program.

The objective is applied: derive principles from consumer and organizational behavior that translate into better-designed technologies, better-trained professionals, and measurable improvements in operational efficiency and safety outcomes.

In plain terms: we surveyed 269 aviation safety professionals worldwide and asked what makes them trust — or not trust — AI tools in their investigations. One factor explained nearly half of all the variation in trust levels. It wasn't age, it wasn't seniority, it wasn't the organization they worked for. It was how professionally engaged they were with their field.

Core Implication for ISASI Guidance

"Organizational factors are non-significant. Trust calibration programs cannot rely on top-down institutional interventions alone — professional engagement is the lever."

Professional engagement interventions work broadly across all subgroups. Management/Safety roles show the strongest effect and represent a priority cohort for early adoption programs.

Composite Trust Model · ISASI 2025 Denver

Presented at ISASI Annual Seminar 2025: AI trust among aviation professionals is not a single variable. It is a dynamic composite of five distinct psychological mechanisms — each requiring a different intervention strategy.

+Curiosity — exploring AI out of genuine interest
+Learned risk — calibrated wariness from past technology failures
+Perceived utility — confidence the tool improves investigative outcomes
+Structural access — organizational inclusion in AI-enabled investigations
+Message framing — whether AI is presented as innovation vs. error prevention

Research Lab Leadership

Running the Infrastructure That Makes Behavioral Research Possible

Directing a research lab at scale is itself a scientific problem. The decisions that shape whether faculty can run rigorous studies — how participant pools are structured, when online experimentation serves a question and when in-person does, how archival and field data needs differ from lab studies, which technology platforms support which research designs — require deep methodological fluency, not just operational management skill.

The Marshall Behavioral Research Lab has grown from a two-department facility into USC Marshall's primary behavioral science infrastructure — supporting 60 active faculty study teams across 11 departments. I lead a team of three full-time staff and serve as the primary methodological partner for faculty and doctoral researchers who need more than a room and a scheduling system.

A significant part of that expertise is understanding the tradeoffs between online and in-person experimentation. The lab navigated a full pivot to remote methodology during COVID-19 — building out Qualtrics, Prolific, and online panel infrastructure — then deliberately shifted back toward in-person as AI-generated responses and automated participation degraded data quality in online panels. That decision required scientific judgment, not just operational adjustment.

"The question of whether a study should run online or in person is not logistical — it is scientific. The answer shapes what you can validly conclude. Getting that right, consistently, across 60 concurrent faculty study teams, is what running a research lab at this scale actually requires."

Operational Adaptation

From Remote-First to In-Person — A Deliberate Strategic Shift

2020 – 2022 · Remote-First

During COVID-19, the lab pivoted entirely to online and remote experimentation — rebuilding participation infrastructure around Qualtrics, Prolific, and third-party online panels. This allowed research to continue uninterrupted and expanded the lab's online methodology capabilities substantially.

2023 – Present · In-Person Direction

As AI-generated responses, automated bots, and data quality degradation became endemic to online panels, the lab shifted strategic direction back toward in-person experimentation — where participant identity and behavioral authenticity can be verified directly. In-person research hours grew 122% in FY2025.

This shift is not a retreat from online methods — third-party and compensated online panels remain part of the lab's methodology portfolio. It reflects a deliberate reallocation of resources toward study designs where data quality can be controlled. The same dynamic is examined in the featured essay on AI trust and in-person research.

Trust & Automation

Why AI Is Driving More Demand for In-Person Research — And What That Tells Us About Trust

Something counterintuitive is happening in behavioral research: as AI becomes more capable of completing online studies, researchers are increasingly turning back to in-person lab environments. The reason is straightforward but underappreciated — they need to be confident they are studying real human participants.

This shift is not primarily about methodology. It is about trust. Specifically, it is about the conditions under which a researcher can trust that the data they collect reflects genuine human cognition rather than automated response. When that trust is uncertain, the entire evidentiary foundation of behavioral science becomes shaky.

From where I sit — directing a research lab that has seen in-person study hours jump 122% in a single year — this looks less like a logistical trend and more like a behavioral signal. It reveals something important about how trust in automated systems actually works: when the stakes are high enough, people revert to methods they can verify directly, even when those methods are more costly and inconvenient.

This is the core problem of automation trust. We don't need people to distrust AI — we need them to trust it appropriately. That means having clear signals for when AI output is reliable, when it isn't, and how to tell the difference. The behavioral science of trust calibration has a lot to say here. Aviation figured this out decades ago. Research methodology is learning it now.

AI Trust Among Aviation Safety Professionals — SEM Study
ISASI Human Factors Working Group needs assessment · N=269 · 49 countries
2024
Structural equation modeling study (EFA→CFA→SEM) identifying professional engagement as the dominant predictor of AI trust among aviation safety investigators (β=0.727, R²=48.9%, p<0.001). Presented at ISASI Annual Seminar 2024 (Lisbon, Portugal) and ISASI Annual Seminar 2025 (Denver, CO). Introduced composite trust model — curiosity, learned risk, perceived utility, structural access, and message framing — as distinct psychological mechanisms each requiring different intervention strategies.
