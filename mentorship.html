He is not a tutoring company or a test prep service. He works with a limited number of students per term and focuses on building durable skills — the kind that follow a student into graduate school, their first job, and beyond.

What to Expect

Real work. Not busy work.

Students who work with Brian do real things. They learn by doing, not by watching. Here is what that looks like in practice.

01
Design actual studies
Students learn how to formulate a research question, choose the right method to test it, and anticipate what can go wrong. This is the skill that separates strong thinkers from everyone else.
02
Collect and handle real data
Students learn how to manage data collection, quality-check responses, and prepare clean datasets for analysis — not just read about it in a textbook.
03
Read research critically
Students learn to read empirical papers the way a researcher does — identifying what the study actually shows, what it cannot show, and where the argument is strong or weak.
04
Write with precision
Academic writing, research proposals, thesis chapters, analytical reports. The goal is writing that makes a clear argument and backs it up — the kind of writing that gets noticed in applications and on the job.
05
Navigate quantitative methods
From descriptive statistics to structural equation modeling, students build genuine analytical fluency — enough to run a study, interpret results honestly, and know when a method is being misapplied.
06
Publish original work
For students ready to go further, Brian guides the full publication process — developing an original research question, writing to journal standards, responding to peer review, and seeing the work through to acceptance.

Is This a Good Fit?

Brian works with students who are ready to work.

He is not the right fit for every student — and he is upfront about that. Here is an honest picture of who tends to thrive, and who tends not to.

Good fit
  • Undergraduates serious about graduate school (PhD, MBA, law, medicine)
  • Students working on a thesis or independent research project
  • Students who want research experience beyond what a class can offer
  • Graduate students who need help with quantitative methods or academic writing
  • Students who are curious and willing to be challenged
  • Students who want honest feedback, not just encouragement
Less likely to be a fit
  • Students looking for someone to do the work for them
  • Students who only want help raising a grade in a specific class
  • Students looking for last-minute help the week before a deadline
  • Students who are not genuinely interested in the substance of research

Common Questions

What families usually want to know.

Does my student need to attend a specific school?
No. Private mentorship engagements are open to high school, undergraduate, and graduate students regardless of institution. Brian has worked with high school students all the way through the publication process — two have had papers accepted or published in peer-reviewed academic journals.
What year of study is best?
There is no single right answer. High school students can start early enough to develop strong research habits before college applications. Undergraduates benefit most when they have time to build skills before graduation — sophomore or junior year is ideal, though seniors and graduate students are welcome. The right time is when the student is genuinely ready to commit to the work.
What does private mentorship look like?
It depends on what the student needs. Common engagements include: guiding original research toward publication, help designing or writing a thesis, preparation for graduate school applications and writing samples, working through quantitative methods for a research project, or building stronger academic reading and writing habits over a semester. Brian works with a limited number of students per term and prioritizes those with an active project to work on.
Is this right for a student who struggles academically?
Possibly, but it depends on the nature of the struggle. If a student finds coursework hard because they have not yet learned how to think analytically — how to read an argument, evaluate evidence, and write with precision — then yes, this work directly addresses that. If the issue is primarily test-taking or course-specific content review, there are better-suited resources for that.
